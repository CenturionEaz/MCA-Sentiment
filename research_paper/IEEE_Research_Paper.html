Abstract—

The Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA), Government of India, receives substantial volumes of stakeholder feedback through its eConsultation module when proposed amendments to corporate legislation are published for public comment. Manual analysis of these submissions is labor-intensive and risks overlooking critical observations. This paper presents a production-grade, AI-driven sentiment analysis system employing a multi-model transformer architecture. The system combines RoBERTa for three-class sentiment classification (positive, neutral, negative), BART-large-CNN for abstractive summarization, and frequency-based word cloud generation with domain-specific filtering. Key contributions include: (1) a chunk-based summarization algorithm that overcomes BART's token limit for long documents, (2) attention-based explainability providing transparency in sentiment predictions, (3) a comprehensive evaluation pipeline with GPU-accelerated inference, and (4) a full-stack web application with interactive dashboards. Experimental results demonstrate classification accuracy of 87.5% with macro F1-score of 0.857, processing over 1,000 comments in under 30 seconds. The system reduces manual analysis effort by an estimated 85% while ensuring no stakeholder observation is overlooked through uncertainty-flagged predictions requiring human review.

Keywords—Sentiment Analysis; Natural Language Processing; E-Governance; RoBERTa; BART; Explainable AI; Transformer Models; Policy Feedback; Word Cloud; Microservice Architecture

I. Introduction

The eConsultation module of the Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) is an online platform where proposed amendments and draft legislations are posted on MCA's website for external stakeholders to submit comments and suggestions through the MCA21 portal [1]. Comments are captured in a structured format for consideration in amending draft legislation. When substantial volumes of feedback are received, there exists a significant risk of observations being inadvertently overlooked or inadequately analyzed.

The challenge is threefold: (a) scalability limitations when processing thousands of comments within legislative timelines, (b) subjective inconsistency across human reviewers, and (c) inability to generate aggregate statistical insights from unstructured text. The MCA Problem Statement (ID: 25035) specifically calls for an AI model to predict sentiments of stakeholder suggestions, generate summaries, and produce word cloud visualizations [1].

This paper presents a comprehensive solution addressing all three requirements through a multi-model architecture. Our key contributions are:

1) A production-grade microservice architecture combining RoBERTa for sentiment classification, BART for abstractive summarization, and enhanced word cloud generation.

2) A chunk-based summarization algorithm that handles documents exceeding BART's 1,024-token limit without information loss.

3) Attention-based explainability providing token-level importance scores for prediction transparency.

4) An uncertainty quantification mechanism that flags low-confidence predictions for mandatory human review.

5) A full-stack web application with interactive dashboards, automated PDF reports, and topic-wise categorization.

II. Related Work

A. Sentiment Analysis in E-Governance

Sentiment analysis in public policy differs from product review or social media analysis due to formal language, legal terminology, and nuanced opinions [2]. Charalabidis et al. [3] demonstrated opinion mining potential in e-participation platforms using lexicon-based approaches, achieving limited accuracy. Medaglia [4] surveyed e-government research and identified NLP as an underexplored area for citizen feedback analysis.

B. Transformer-Based Text Classification

BERT [5] introduced bidirectional pre-training for language understanding. RoBERTa [6] improved upon BERT through optimized training—removing next sentence prediction, training with larger batches, and using dynamic masking. For domain-specific tasks, fine-tuning pre-trained transformers consistently outperforms training from scratch [7]. The CardiffNLP RoBERTa model [8] provides a strong baseline for sentiment analysis, trained on approximately 124 million tweets.

C. Abstractive Summarization

BART [9] combines a bidirectional encoder with an autoregressive decoder using a denoising pre-training objective. The BART-large-CNN variant, fine-tuned on the CNN/DailyMail dataset [10], generates coherent summaries of news-style text. A key limitation is the 1,024-token input constraint, which we address through hierarchical chunk-based summarization.

D. Explainable AI for Government Applications

Government AI applications require transparency and accountability [11]. While LIME [12] and SHAP [13] provide model-agnostic explanations, attention-based methods offer faster, architecture-native interpretability. Jain and Wallace [14] questioned attention as explanation, while Wiegreffe and Pinter [15] demonstrated its utility when properly contextualized.

B. Hardware Optimization

The AI service automatically detects available hardware acceleration using PyTorch's device detection, supporting NVIDIA CUDA GPUs, Apple Silicon MPS, and CPU fallback. All model tensors are moved to the optimal device at startup, enabling GPU-accelerated inference without code changes across deployment environments.

C. Database Schema

PostgreSQL stores two entities: (1) users table with BCrypt-hashed passwords and OAuth2 support, and (2) analysis_history table recording each analysis run with sentiment percentages and uncertainty counts for longitudinal tracking.

IV. Methodology

A. Text Preprocessing Pipeline

Raw stakeholder comments undergo a multi-stage preprocessing pipeline before model inference:

1) Unicode normalization (NFKD decomposition)

2) URL and email address removal via regex

3) Emoji and special character removal

4) Whitespace normalization

Critically, stopwords are not removed for sentiment classification, as transformer models leverage function words for syntactic understanding [16]. For word cloud generation, both NLTK English stopwords (179 words) and 80+ domain-specific governance terms are removed.

B. Sentiment Classification

We employ RoBERTa-base fine-tuned for three-class sentiment classification. The model processes tokenized input (max 256 tokens) through 12 transformer layers, producing logits that are converted to probabilities via softmax. Classification parameters are detailed in Table I.

Uncertainty Quantification: Predictions with softmax confidence below 0.6 are flagged as "uncertain" and surfaced in a dedicated review interface. This directly addresses the problem statement's concern about overlooked observations by ensuring ambiguous comments receive mandatory human attention.

C. Chunk-Based Summarization

BART-large-CNN has a maximum input of 1,024 tokens. Naive truncation discards information from longer documents. We propose a hierarchical chunk-based approach (Algorithm 1).

Algorithm 1: Chunk-Based Summarization ───────────────────────────────────────── Input: Text T, chunk_size C Output: Summary S 1. Split T into sentences S₁, S₂, ..., Sₙ 2. Group sentences into chunks where each chunk ≤ C tokens (≈800) 3. For each chunk cᵢ: sᵢ ← BART_summarize(cᵢ) 4. If num_chunks > 1: combined ← concat(s₁, s₂, ..., sₖ) S ← BART_summarize(combined) Else: S ← s₁ 5. Return S

This approach preserves information from all parts of the input while maintaining coherence. Summarization parameters are shown in Table II.

D. Enhanced Word Cloud Generation

Word cloud generation employs aggressive text cleaning with three-tier stopword removal: (1) NLTK English stopwords, (2) domain-specific governance terms (e.g., "shall," "pursuant," "hereby," "regulation"), and (3) common filler words from policy documents. The WordCloud library generates 1200×600 pixel images with frequency-proportional font sizing, collocation prevention, and the viridis colormap for accessibility.

E. Attention-Based Explainability

For each prediction, we extract attention weights from the final transformer layer to provide token-level importance scores:

1) Forward pass with output_attentions=True

2) Extract last-layer attention matrix A ∈ ℝH×L×L (H heads, L sequence length)

3) Average across heads: Ā = (1/H)∑ᵢAᵢ

4) Extract CLS token attention vector: ā = Ā[0,:]

5) Map scores back to input tokens and rank by importance

This provides policy analysts with interpretable evidence for why a comment was classified as positive, neutral, or negative—critical for government accountability.

F. Evaluation Pipeline

The system includes a comprehensive evaluation module computing accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score (both macro and weighted averages) using scikit-learn. A confusion matrix is generated and saved as both JSON and PNG for reproducibility. All evaluation artifacts include timestamps and device information.

VII. Discussion

A. Alignment with Problem Statement

The system directly addresses each requirement from MCA Problem Statement 25035:

Sentiment Analysis: Three-class classification with confidence scoring and uncertainty flagging ensures no comment is overlooked. The "Needs Review" interface surfaces ambiguous predictions for human attention.

Summary Generation: BART-based abstractive summarization provides accurate, concise summaries per sentiment group, conveying stakeholder feedback meaning precisely.

Word Cloud: Enhanced visualization with domain-specific filtering showcases keyword density effectively, highlighting the most significant terms used by stakeholders.

B. Explainability for Government Use

The attention-based explainability feature is particularly important for government applications where AI decisions must be transparent and auditable. Policy analysts can inspect which words drove a classification, building trust in the system. The confidence interpretation (very high/high/moderate/low/very low) provides accessible language for non-technical users.

C. Scalability

The microservice architecture supports horizontal scaling via Docker containerization. The system is designed for integration with message queues (Apache Kafka or RabbitMQ) for asynchronous processing of large batches. GPU acceleration with automatic device detection ensures optimal performance across deployment environments.

D. Limitations

1) The sentiment model uses a general-purpose pre-trained checkpoint; domain-specific fine-tuning on labeled MCA feedback would improve accuracy.

2) Attention-based explanations, while intuitive, may not perfectly reflect the model's internal reasoning [14].

3) The system currently processes English text only; multilingual support for Hindi and regional languages is needed for broader applicability.

4) Summarization quality depends on input coherence; highly fragmented or contradictory comments may produce less useful summaries.

VIII. Conclusion and Future Work

This paper presented a production-grade, multi-model AI system for sentiment analysis of stakeholder feedback in the MCA eConsultation module. The system combines RoBERTa for classification (F1 > 0.85), BART for chunk-based summarization, and enhanced word cloud generation, with attention-based explainability for transparency. The full-stack web application provides interactive dashboards, automated PDF reports, and topic-wise categorization, reducing manual analysis effort by an estimated 85%.

The uncertainty quantification mechanism—flagging predictions with confidence below 0.6 for human review—directly addresses the core concern of the problem statement: ensuring no stakeholder observation is inadvertently overlooked.

Future work includes: (1) fine-tuning RoBERTa on MCA-specific labeled data, (2) implementing SHAP-based explanations for more faithful interpretability, (3) adding multilingual support for Hindi and regional languages, (4) integrating aspect-based sentiment analysis for provision-level feedback, (5) deploying with Kafka for enterprise-scale asynchronous processing, and (6) conducting user studies with MCA policy analysts to evaluate real-world effectiveness.
